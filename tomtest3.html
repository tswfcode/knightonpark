Parks

This extremely beautiful 78 acre park has colourful shrub borders, pond, rock garden, two well-equipped play areas and a nine-hole pitch and putt golf course which is open during the holidays. Trees are a strong feature of the park with avenues of laburnum close to the Spinney, gingko (maidenhair) avenue in the Heath Garden and a charming path bordered by magnolias across the middle of the park. In addition to the Spinney there are newly planted lime woods, a beech wood and willow wood.

Playgrounds

Knighton Park has two playgrounds, each being suitable for different age groups. The toddler playground is great for younger children that require parental supervision as it comprises of safety swings, a sand pit and low to the ground climbing frames. Most equipment is surrounded by large wood-chip grounding and there are several benches and picnic tables. The larger playground is suitable for older children and has larger swings, a large free hanging tire swing, monkey bars and high frame climbing equipment. Both play areas are fenced and dogs are not permitted inside.

Activities

As well as the playgrounds Knighton Park has various other activities on offer. There is an open air gym which includes an array of resistance machines covering endurance, toning and cardiovascular fitness. In addition to the gym there are also 2 football pitches, 2 hard surface tennis courts with nets, 3 orienteering courses and a table tennis table (don't forget your bats and balls). During the summer there is also a 9 hole pitch and put course.

Knighton Park History

Knighton village appears in the Domesday Book under its Scandinavian name of Cnihetone. In the 1720’s, Edmund Cradock, a woollen draper of Leicester bought Knighton Hall and its associated farmland. In the years that followed, the family acquired almost all the land in the parish. Knighton Hall is now the official residence of the Vice Chancellor of Leicester University and the name of the Cradock’s is preserved in the partially thatched Cradock pub. The City Council bought land from the Cradock-Hartopp trustees just before the Second World War with the intention of establishing a park but work was not begun until 1953.
